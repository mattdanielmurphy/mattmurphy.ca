Inquiry Question

A bad traffic accident has taken place between a motorcycle and a car. Can you interpret the skid marks and wreckage to reconstruct the initial conditions? Was the motorcycle speeding?

Procedure and Necessary Data

To recreate the accident, I will use Classical Newtonian Mechanics, specifically the Work-Energy Theorem (kinematics/friction) and the Law of Conservation of Momentum.

Procedure:

  1. Use the known length of the car to establish a scale for the diagram and measure the distances of the skid marks (d1​) and wreckage slide (d2​).
  2. Use the Work-Energy Theorem (or equations of motion) to calculate the deceleration of both the braking bike and the sliding wreckage using estimated friction coefficients.
  3. Calculate the speed of the wreckage immediately after impact by working backward from its final resting spot.
  4. Use the Conservation of Momentum (with vector components) to determine the speed of the motorcycle immediately before impact.
  5. Use kinematics to calculate the initial speed of the motorcycle before the rider hit the brakes.

Part 1: Core

1. Collision Classification and Energy Conservation

This is a perfectly inelastic collision because the motorcycle became embedded into the BMW, and the two vehicles moved together as a single unit after the impact. While total energy is always conserved in the universe, the Kinetic Energy of the system is NOT conserved during the impact. Some of the initial kinetic energy was transformed into heat, sound, and the severe physical deformation of the metal during the collision.

2. Momentum as a Vector Quantity

Momentum is a VECTOR quantity, and the total momentum vector of a system is conserved provided no significant external impulse acts during the brief time of impact.

System defined: The system consists of the 1991 Suzuki GSXR1100 with rider and the 1972 BMW 3.0 CSL with driver, analyzed immediately before and immediately after the impact.

Part 3: Ace - Finalizing your report

1. The bike/car skid to the curb (tolerance of ∣vf,bike/car​∣)

The initial speed of the wreckage depends heavily on my assumed coefficient of friction (μk​=0.4). If the road was rougher, my calculation would be larger. If the surface was smoother (μk​=0.3), the wreckage velocity drops to 6.54 m/s. If it was rougher (μk​=0.5), it rises to 8.44 m/s. Therefore, the range for ∣vf,bike/car​∣ falls between 6.5 m/s and 8.4 m/s.

2. Effect of tolerance on ∣vi,bike​∣

Because the final momentum of the wreckage dictates the required momentum of the system, a larger value for ∣vf,bike/car​∣ results in a larger calculated value for ∣vi,bike​∣. If I apply the velocity range from Step 1 (6.54 m/s to 8.44 m/s) backward through the vector components, the initial speed of the bike immediately before the collision changes accordingly. The estimated range for ∣vi,bike​∣ would be between 31.1 m/s and 40.1 m/s.

3. Effect on initial speed before brakes and final determination

If the bike was traveling faster at impact, it was also traveling faster before the brakes were applied. Using the kinematic equation from Part 2.5 with the new range from Step 2, the initial speed of the bike before braking ranges from 36.7 m/s (132 km/h) to 44.6 m/s (161 km/h).

The measurement of the 27.7 m skid mark is also best treated as a minimum visible braking distance. Motorcycles can begin slowing down before a clearly visible skid mark appears, so the actual braking distance may have been longer than 27.7 m. That means this reconstruction likely gives a conservative estimate of the motorcycle's original speed. Even at the lowest end of the tolerance range (∼132 km/h), the physics reconstruction provides strong evidence that the motorcycle was speeding prior to the collision.
